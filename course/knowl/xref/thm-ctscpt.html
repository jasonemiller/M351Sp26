Theorem 218. Preservation of Compact Sets.
Let \(f : A \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) be continuous on \(A\text{.}\) If \(K \subseteq A\) is compact, then \(f(K)\) is compact as well.
🔗
🔗
Proof.
Suppose that \(K \subseteq A\) is compact. We want to show that
\begin{gather*} f(K) = \{f(y) \, : \, y \in K \} \end{gather*}
is compact. We will show that \(f(K)\) is sequentially compact.
🔗
Let \((y_n)\) be a sequence in \(f(K)\text{.}\) We must show that it has a subsequence that converges to a limit in \(f(K)\text{.}\)
🔗
Fix \(k \in \mathbb{N}\text{.}\) Since \(y_k \in f(K)\) there is an \(x \in K\) with the property that \(f(x)=y_k\text{.}\) Call this element of \(x=x_k\text{.}\) We can find such an element for each \(k \in \mathbb{N}\text{.}\) This gives us a sequence \((x_n) \subset K\text{.}\) Now we can use information about \(K\) begin compact.
🔗
Since \(K\) is compact, \((x_n)\) has a convergent subsequence \((x_{n_k})\text{.}\) Let \(x_0\) be the limit of this subsequence. Since \(K\) is compact, \(x_0 \in K\text{.}\) Therefore, \(f(x_0) \in f(K)\text{.}\)
🔗
At the same time, \((f(x_{n_k}))\) is a subsequence of \((y_n) \subset f(K)\text{.}\) Moreover, because \(f\) is continuous on \(K\text{,}\) the sequence \(f(x_{n_k}) \subset f(K)\) converges to \(f(x_0)\text{.}\) Thus, our subsequence converges in \(f(K)\text{.}\) Therefore, \(f(K)\) is compact, as desired.
