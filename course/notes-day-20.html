    Section Showing Continuity by Hand

    Last time, we proved using the definition that a linear function is continuous at a point, and we showed it is continuous on all of \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We also proved using the definition that the squaring function \(y=x^2\) is continuous at a point, and we showed it is continuous on all of \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We could do the same for the square root function \(y=\sqrt{y}\text{,}\) showing that it is continuous on its domain of all reals \(x \geq 0\text{.}\) A similar proof will show that \(y=\sqrt[n]{y}\) is continuous for natural numbers \(n \geq 2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Using the definition to show that the cosine function is continuous at \(x=c\) uses the following facts:
    1. \(\displaystyle \cos(x)-\cos(c) = -2 \sin\left( \frac{x+c}{2} \right) \sin \left( \frac{x-c}{2} \right)\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\displaystyle | \sin \theta | \leq 1\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \(\displaystyle | \sin \theta | \leq |\theta|\)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Because \(x=c\) is arbitrary, the proof can show that the cosine function is continuous on all reals.
      🔗
      🔗

    Section Continuous Functions

    Last time we proved that a linear function is continuous on all of \(\mathbb{R}\text{,}\) and we proved that the squaring function is, too. The proofs revealed something about how the choice of \(\delta\) depends (or doesn’t) on both \(\epsilon\) and the points at which we’re establishing continuity, i.e., \(x=c\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We will see that, as a consequence of the Algebraic Continuity Theorem, polynomials are continuous functions on all of \(\mathbb{R}\)
    🔗
    The same theorem will tell us that rational functions (which are quotients of polynomials) are continuous on their domain (which is all of \(\mathbb{R}\) minus the finite number of points at which their denominator vanishes).
    🔗
    We’d like to know that other elementary functions are continuous on their domains, too. These include
    • algebraic functions
      🔗
      🔗
    • trigonometric functions
      🔗
      🔗
    • exponential functions
      🔗
      🔗
    • logarithm functions
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Other special functions, such as the hyperbolic trigonometric functions, are algebraic combinations of the above. Others, like the Normal (a.k.a., Gaussian) probability distribution function, is a functional composition of the above.
    🔗
    We won’t go through the details for exponential functions, for example, but we can show from first principles that the exponential functions are continuous on their domain.
    🔗
    We have other theorems that will help us prove the other functions are continuous without relying on the definition.
    🔗
    🔗

    Section Algebraic Continuity Theorem

    🔗
    Theorem 216.
    Assume \(f : A → \mathbb{R}\) and \(g : A → \mathbb{R}\) are continuous at a point \(c \in A\text{.}\) Then,
    1. \(kf(x)\) is continuous at \(c\) for all \(k \in \mathbb{R}\text{;}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(f(x)+g(x)\) is continuous at \(c\text{;}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \(f(x)g(x)\) is continuous at \(c\text{;}\) and
      🔗
      🔗
    4. \(f(x)/g(x)\) is continuous at \(c\text{,}\) provided the quotient is defined.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Proof.
    Let’s prove (Item 2).
    🔗
    Let \((x_n) \subset A\) be a sequence that converges to \(c\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Then because \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) are continuous at \(c\text{,}\) the sequences \((f(x_n))\) and \((g(x_n))\) converge to \(f(c)\) and \(g(c)\text{,}\) respectively.
    🔗
    By the Algebraic Limit Theorem for sequences, we know \((f(x_n)+g(x_n))\) converges to \(f(c)+g(c)=(f+g)(c)\text{.}\) Therefore, by the sequential characterization of functional limits, we can conclude that \((f(x)+g(x))\) converges to \(f(c)+g(c)=(f+g)(c)\) as \(x \rightarrow c\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The other items in the theorem statement are provides analogously.
    🔗
    🔗
    With this (and some of our previous by-hand proofs) we can, with induction, show all polynomials are continuous on their domain. And all rational functions. And all trigonometric functions. And all algebraic functions.
    🔗
    Lots of functions.
    🔗
    🔗

    Section Composition of Continuous Functions

    This next theorem increases the variety of functions that we can say are continuous, such as these:
    1. \(\displaystyle \sqrt{x^2+x+1}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\displaystyle \cos(x^2)\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \(\displaystyle e^{-x^2}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Theorem 217. Composition of Continuous Functions.
    Given \(f : A \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) and \(g : B \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\text{,}\) assume that the range \(f(A) = \{f(x) : x \in A\}\) is contained in the domain \(B\) so that the composition \(g \circ f(x) = g(f(x))\) is defined on \(A\text{.}\) If \(f\) is continuous at \(c \in A\text{,}\) and if \(g\) is continuous at \(f(c) \in B\text{,}\) then \(g \circ f\) is continuous at \(c\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Proof.
    Easy proof if you use the sequential characterization of continuity at a point.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Section Continuous functions and Compact Sets

    Theorem 218. Preservation of Compact Sets.
    Let \(f : A \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) be continuous on \(A\text{.}\) If \(K \subseteq A\) is compact, then \(f(K)\) is compact as well.
    🔗
    🔗
    Proof.
    Suppose that \(K \subseteq A\) is compact. We want to show that
    \begin{gather*} f(K) = \{f(y) \, : \, y \in K \} \end{gather*}
    is compact. We will show that \(f(K)\) is sequentially compact.
    🔗
    Let \((y_n)\) be a sequence in \(f(K)\text{.}\) We must show that it has a subsequence that converges to a limit in \(f(K)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Fix \(k \in \mathbb{N}\text{.}\) Since \(y_k \in f(K)\) there is an \(x \in K\) with the property that \(f(x)=y_k\text{.}\) Call this element of \(x=x_k\text{.}\) We can find such an element for each \(k \in \mathbb{N}\text{.}\) This gives us a sequence \((x_n) \subset K\text{.}\) Now we can use information about \(K\) begin compact.
    🔗
    Since \(K\) is compact, \((x_n)\) has a convergent subsequence \((x_{n_k})\text{.}\) Let \(x_0\) be the limit of this subsequence. Since \(K\) is compact, \(x_0 \in K\text{.}\) Therefore, \(f(x_0) \in f(K)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    At the same time, \((f(x_{n_k}))\) is a subsequence of \((y_n) \subset f(K)\text{.}\) Moreover, because \(f\) is continuous on \(K\text{,}\) the sequence \(f(x_{n_k}) \subset f(K)\) converges to \(f(x_0)\text{.}\) Thus, our subsequence converges in \(f(K)\text{.}\) Therefore, \(f(K)\) is compact, as desired.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Section Extreme Value Theorem

    Theorem 219. Extreme Value Theorem.
    If \(f : K \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) is continuous on a compact set \(K \subset \mathbb{R}\text{,}\) then \(f\) attains a maximum and minimum value. In other words, there exist \(x_0, x_1 \in K\) such that \(f(x_0) \leq f(x) \leq f(x_1)\) for all \(x \in K\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Proof.
    Suppose \(f\) is continuous on a compact set \(K \subset \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) By Theorem 218, \(f(K)\) is compact. Therefore, because \(f(K)\) is closed and bounded, it contains the two limit points \(\alpha = \sup(f(K))\) and \(\beta=\inf(f(K))\text{.}\) Since \(\alpha \in f(K)\text{,}\) there is an \(x_0 \in K\) for which \(f(x_0)=\inf f(K)\text{.}\) Since \(\beta \in f(K)\text{,}\) there is an \(x_1 \in K\) for which \(f(x_1)=\sup f(K)\text{.}\) Therefore,

    Section Uniform Continuity

    Our definition of continuity (see Definition 209) expresses the idea that as \(x\) gets closer and closer to \(c\text{,}\) the value of \(f(x)\) gets closer and closer to the value \(f(c)\text{.}\) This means that we can make \(|f(x)-f(c)|\) as small as we want by making \(|x-c|\) small enough.
    🔗
    We’ve seen through examples that how small we make \(|x-c|\) can depend both on \(\epsilon\) and \(c\text{.}\)
    1. In our proof of continuity of \(f(x)=7x+5\) we saw that \(\delta=\epsilon/7\) works. Notice: no dependence on \(c\text{!}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. In a proof of continuity of \(f(x)=\sqrt{x}\) at a nonzero \(x=c\) we would see that \(\delta=\epsilon \cdot \sqrt{c}\) works. Here, if \(|c|\) is small, then it it forces \(\delta\) to be smaller!
      🔗
      🔗
    3. In our proof of continuity of \(f(x)=x^2\) we saw that \(\delta=\epsilon/(2|c|+1))\) works. Here, if \(|c|\) is big, then it forces \(\delta\) to be smaller.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    For some functions, the point at which we are working (i.e., \(x=c\)) plays an important role (viz a viz \(f(x)=x^2\)).
    🔗
    For other functions (e.g., \(f(x)=\sqrt{x}\) and \(f(x)=7x+5\)), where we are examining continuity plays less of a role.
    🔗
    🔗
    Definition 220.
    A function \(f : A \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) is uniformly continuous on \(A\) if for every \(\epsilon > 0\) there exists a \(\delta > 0\) such that for all \(x,y \in A\) with \(|x-y| < \delta\) we know \(|f(x)-f(y)| < \epsilon\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Section Uniformly Continuous Functions

    Theorem 221. Uniform Continuity on Compact Sets.
    A function that is continuous on a compact set \(K\) is uniformly continuous on \(K\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Proof.
    Let \(\epsilon > 0\) be given. Since \(f\) is continuous on \(K\text{,}\) for each \(c \in K\) there is a \(\delta = \delta(c) > 0\) for which \(x \in V_{\delta(c)}(c)\) implies \(f(x) \in V_{\frac{1}{2}\epsilon}(f(c))\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Note that \(V_{\frac{1}{2} \delta(c)}(c)\) is the set of all \(x \in K\) for which \(|x-c| < \frac{1}{2}\delta(c)\text{.}\) The collection
    \begin{align*} \mathcal{U} \amp = \{ V_{\frac{1}{2} \delta(c)}(c) \, : \, c \in K \} \end{align*}
    is an open cover of \(K\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Since \(K\) is compact, \(\mathcal{U}\) contains a finite subcover \(\mathcal{U}_0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    This means that there are a finite number of points \(c_i \in K\text{,}\) where \(i=1,2,3,\dots,n\)such that
    \begin{gather*} K \subset V_{\frac{1}{2} \delta(c_1)}(c_1) \cup V_{\frac{1}{2} \delta(c_2)}(c_2) \cup \cdots \cup V_{\frac{1}{2} \delta(c_n)}(c_n) \cup \end{gather*}
    🔗
    Define \(\delta = \frac{1}{2} \min \{ \delta(c_i) \, : \, i=1,2,3,\ldots,n\}\text{.}\) Then \(\delta > 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Let \(x,y \in K\) such that \(|x-y| < \delta\text{.}\) Since \(\mathcal{U}_0\) is an open cover, there is a \(1 \leq m \leq n\) for which \(x \in V_{\frac{1}{2} \delta(c_m)}(c_m)\text{.}\) That means \(|x-c_m| < \frac{1}{2} \delta(c_m)\text{.}\) We also have
    \begin{align*} |y-c_m| \amp = |y-x+x-c_m| \\ \amp \leq |y-x|+|x-c_m| \\ \amp < \delta + \frac{1}{2} \delta(c_m)\\ \amp < \delta(c_m) \end{align*}
    It follows that
    \begin{align*} |f(x)-f(y)| \amp = |f(x)-f(c_m)+f(c_m)-f(y)|\\ |\amp \leq |f(x)-f(c_m)|+|f(c_m)-f(y)|\\ \amp < \epsilon/2 | \epsilon/2 \\ \amp = \epsilon \end{align*}
    🔗
    This shows that \(f\) is uniformly continuous on \(K\text{.}\)
